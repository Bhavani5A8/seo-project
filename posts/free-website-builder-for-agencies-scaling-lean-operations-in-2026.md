## FAQ: Technical Questions Answered

**Q1: Can a free website builder truly handle the traffic of a scaling agency?**

Yes, provided the builder is built on top of a scalable cloud infrastructure like AWS or Google Cloud. The misconception that "free" means "unreliable" is a holdover from the early days of the web. Modern freemium platforms use the same global CDN (Content Delivery Network) nodes for their free users as they do for their enterprise clients. The limitation is usually on features (like custom domains or branding) rather than performance. From a technical standpoint, a site built on a reputable builder can handle thousands of concurrent visitors because the "static" nature of the pages requires minimal server-side processing compared to a dynamic WordPress site.

**Q2: How do I handle 301 redirects when moving from a legacy site to a lean builder?**

Managing 301 redirects is the most critical part of an agency migration. Most lean builders provide a simple CSV upload tool for redirects. You must map every single legacy URL to its new counterpart. If you miss this step, you will lose your accumulated "link equity," and your rankings will plummet. Before flipping the switch, use a tool like Screaming Frog to crawl your old site and generate a complete list of URLs. Once migrated, monitor your Google Search Console for "404 Not Found" errors and patch them immediately to ensure a seamless transition for both users and crawlers.

**Q4: Is it possible to maintain high rankings with a subdomain instead of a root domain?**

In 2026, the "Subdomain vs. Subdirectory" debate has been largely settled by Google’s move toward "Entity-Based Indexing." While a root domain is generally preferred for long-term brand building, a well-optimized subdomain can rank just as high for specific service keywords. The key is the "Quality Signal" of the parent domain. When you use a subdomain from a high-authority builder, you often benefit from the "halo effect" of the platform’s overall technical health. For an agency, starting on a subdomain and then mapping a custom domain later is a perfectly viable technical path that doesn't harm SEO.

**Q5: How do lean builders handle "Dynamic Content" like blog posts and case studies?**

Modern builders use a "Headless CMS" approach for dynamic content. This means the content is stored in a database but is "pre-rendered" into static HTML pages whenever you hit "Publish." This gives you the speed of a static site with the ease of use of a blog. From a technical SEO perspective, this is the best of both worlds. You get lightning-fast load times for your long-form content, which is essential for ranking for "How-to" and "What is" keywords that drive top-of-funnel agency traffic without the database lag of traditional CMS platforms.

**Q6: Can I implement custom CSS and JavaScript if my agency needs a specific "look and feel"?**

Most high-end lean builders allow for "Custom Code Injection" in the <head> and <footer> sections. However, the lean philosophy encourages you to limit this. If you must add custom code, ensure it is minified and hosted on a fast CDN. Avoid "Render-Blocking" scripts that stop the page from loading until the script is finished. A Senior SEO Engineer would suggest using the "defer" or "async" attribute on all custom scripts to ensure they don't impact your Core Web Vitals. The goal is to keep the "Core" of the site standardized while adding "Aesthetic Flourishes" as light layers.
