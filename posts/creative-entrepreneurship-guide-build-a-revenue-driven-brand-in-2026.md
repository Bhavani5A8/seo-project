## FAQ: Technical Questions Answered

### Q1: How do I handle large image galleries without hurting my SEO?

The key to handling large galleries lies in &ldquo;Lazy Loading&rdquo; and &ldquo;Responsive Breakpoints.&rdquo; You should never load all images at once. Instead, configure your site to only load images as the user scrolls them into the viewport. Additionally, use the `srcset` attribute in your HTML. This allows the browser to choose the smallest possible image file based on the user&rsquo;s screen size. For example, a mobile user shouldn&rsquo;t download a 2000px wide image meant for a 5K monitor. By serving optimized sizes, you keep your PageSpeed scores high while still showcasing your work in high resolution where it counts. Also, ensure every image has descriptive Alt Text, which helps Google’s &ldquo;Vision AI&rdquo; understand and index your art.

### Q2: What is the best way to track conversions for a creative business?

In 2026, standard &ldquo;page view&rdquo; tracking is insufficient. You need to implement &ldquo;Event-Based Tracking&rdquo; via Google Analytics 4 (GA4). For a creative entrepreneur, key events include &ldquo;Newsletter Sign-ups,&rdquo; &ldquo;Portfolio Download,&rdquo; &ldquo;Contact Form Submission,&rdquo; and &ldquo;Add to Cart.&rdquo; By setting up these custom events, you can see exactly which traffic sources (e.g., Pinterest vs. Organic Search) are driving high-value actions. Furthermore, use &ldquo;Enhanced Measurement&rdquo; to track scroll depth. If users are only seeing the top 25% of your page, you know you need to move your &ldquo;Call to Action&rdquo; buttons higher up to capture interest before they bounce.

### Q4: How do I protect my creative work from being scraped by AI?

This is a major concern in the current era. While you want your work to be found by search engines, you may want to prevent it from being used to train AI models without your consent. You can implement specific directives in your `robots.txt` file to disallow &ldquo;GPTBot&rdquo; and other known AI crawlers. Additionally, consider using &ldquo;No-Index&rdquo; tags on high-resolution gallery pages while allowing indexing on lower-resolution preview pages. Another advanced method is using &ldquo;Cloaking&rdquo; or &ldquo;Watermarking&rdquo; metadata (like the IPTC standard) which embeds ownership information directly into the image file, making it harder for scrapers to strip your identity from your work.

### Q5: Should I use a Subdomain or a Subdirectory for my blog?

From a technical SEO perspective, a subdirectory (e.g., yoursite.com/blog) is almost always superior to a subdomain (e.g., blog.yoursite.com). Search engines generally treat a subdomain as a separate entity, meaning the &ldquo;authority&rdquo; and backlink profile of your main site doesn&rsquo;t automatically benefit your blog, and vice-versa. By using a subdirectory, you consolidate all your SEO power into a single domain. This makes it easier to rank for competitive keywords because every blog post you write adds to the total &ldquo;weight&rdquo; of your primary URL. For creative entrepreneurs, this means your &ldquo;How-To&rdquo; guides and process stories directly help your product pages rank higher.

Security is not just about protection; it is about trust and SEO. Google has officially confirmed that HTTPS is a ranking signal. For a creative entrepreneur, an &ldquo;Insecure&rdquo; warning in a browser is a death sentence for sales.
